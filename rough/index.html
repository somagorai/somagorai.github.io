About Warehouse

Before starting with the case study, setting the context with what how a warehouse works -

Image shows a generic Warehouse (Credits Axacute)

1. Receiving Area (Inbound Warehouse)
What happens? Trucks pull in with new stock or returns. Workers scan each box and put it away on shelves.
A Warehouse Management System keeps a precise list of what’s on every shelf, so nothing gets lost and you always know what’s available.2. Work Area (Shop Floor)
What happens? Shelved items move to packing lines or machines. Here, humans and automatic robots work side by side:
Robots fetch pallets or boxes and deliver them to workstations.
Workers then pick individual items, assemble kits, or load parts into machines.
A Warehouse Execution System coordinates those moves in real time—telling each robot where to go, which box to bring, and tracking how long each task takes—so nothing sits idle or causes delays.3. Shipping Area (Outbound Warehouse)
What happens? Finished packages are picked from shelves, boxed up, and loaded onto outgoing trucks. Staff double-check counts to ensure accuracy.

Why a Control Tower? A gives you a top-down view of every zone. It flags slow spots—say, a backlog at packing—so you can fix them before customers notice.

What is Control Tower?

A Control Tower is a centralized dashboard that sits on top of your warehouse systems (WMS/WES) and brings together data from every zone, robot, workstation, and shipment lane into one real-time view.Why Is It Required?
  • Everything in One Screen
    Brings inventory, robot, and workflow info together so you don’t have to switch between tools.
  • Live Status Updates
    Shows what’s happening right now—where work is piling up or moving smoothly.
  • Early Warnings & Fixes
    Notifies you of slow spots and offers simple steps to clear them before they become big problems.
  • Easy Deep Dives
    Click any number or chart to see the story behind it and learn what to improve.
  • Plug-and-Play Setup
    Works the same way in every warehouse—so you can roll it out quickly without starting from scratch.

Existing Platform

Axon’s team had previously attempted to build a Control Tower application, but the solution fell short both technically and in terms of user experience. The interface lacked clarity, modularity, and the ability to scale, which limited its usability for real-time monitoring and operational decision-making.

Process Map

A process map is a visual representation of the physical processes ongoing in a warehouse. ​
It’s a realtime map of the warehouse processes which would let the users find out bottlenecks.

The process map is one of our key USPs.
(1) It provides an effective overview of the entire warehouse by mapping out each ongoing process.
(2) With real-time reports, it enables tracking of the warehouse's performance and helps identify bottlenecks, their dependencies, and causes.
(3) This allows for timely, informed action to resolve issues and improve efficiency.

Image shows a Warehouse Process Map with bottlenecks highlighted in red.

Creating the process map was a complex task, and we spent considerable time exploring the best way to approach it. After several brainstorming sessions and multiple iterations, we finalized the fourth approach — building the map by adding predefined sections — as it offered the right balance of flexibility, control, and ease of use.

Design System

Once we received the project go-ahead, we began by setting up a design system aligned with other ongoing projects. We created a dual theme — dark and light — to support varied use cases across user types. Most components were built from scratch, as the product demanded highly customized screens and interactions. To maintain visual and functional consistency, we followed design patterns inspired by AG Grid and Material UI, which had already been adopted in related projects.

Full Product Delivery

Once the team was formed (January 1), we began executing the project in a sprint-based model — planned across 12 sprints over 6 months.
The process began with an initial alignment meeting involving the Product Manager, Designers, and Technical Architect, where we revisited the PoC, identified core business objectives, and discussed technical feasibility.
From there, the Product Manager created a working PRD (Product Requirements Document) — a living document that evolved continuously. After every 2–3 sprints, we held review and alignment meetings with the client and SMEs, using their feedback to refine requirements, adjust priorities, and enhance functionality.

As designers, we translated the PRD into actual product experiences by
(1) designing sprint-specific feature sets,
(2) holding design reviews with PMs and developers,
(3) ensuring alignment with technical constraints, and
(4) maintaining visual consistency using our customized design system.

This collaborative, evolving process allowed us to stay agile and user-focused, despite tight timelines.

Quotation Creation Process

Expanding for the Sales Team, we added a new flow specifically for sales teams to generate client estimates. The Quotation serves as the business proposal presented to clients, outlining all warehouse components like robots, storage, stations, and operators.

The Process Map is then generated from an existing Quotation, ensuring seamless tracking and transparency.

Previously, this entire workflow relied on Excel sheets and multiple disconnected software, making integration a key improvement.

The input process was ensured to be as simple and instructive as possible.

Quotation ➡ Process Map ➡ Deployment ➡ Monitoring

There were some major shift in understanding because of technical considerations –

1. Shifted from adding items (Communications, Automation Vendors, Robots) during Process Map creation to fetching them from an existing Quotation. This also aligned with the Business Requirements.

2. Streamlined the process by reducing manual input and ensuring consistency.

3. Improved fault diagnosis by attaching images of robots and devices for easy identification

Impact & Learnings

This project is still evolving, with design and development happening in parallel — a dynamic and fast-paced environment that has offered deep learning opportunities.

(1) Design–Development Sync: Working closely with developers exposed where designs broke down in implementation. Seeing their challenges firsthand helped refine our approach to make interactions and layouts more practical and efficient.

(2) Adapting to Constraints: From technical limitations to shifting client expectations, we had to adjust fast — sometimes rethinking solutions mid-sprint while maintaining usability and visual consistency.

(3) Real-Time Collaboration: Continuous feedback from PMs, SMEs, and developers taught us to value working documents and iterative processes over static handoffs.

(4) Designing for Scale: With business stakeholders aiming to convert this into a scalable product (not a one-off project), we focused on modular design, flexible components, and centralized configurability.

(5) Designing Without End-Users: We didn’t have direct access to end-users, so we relied heavily on SMEs for insight. Learning to ask the right questions and speak in their language helped us gauge what would or wouldn’t work in the real environment.

(6) Learning from Scratch: Coming into this project, we had no prior domain expertise in warehouse operations. The early phases were a crash course in understanding warehouse systems, technical architectures, and industry workflows—essential for making informed design decisions.

Every sprint brought unexpected challenges, but each one pushed us to rethink, simplify, and design better.
